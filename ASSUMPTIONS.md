# Assumptions Log

The assumptions that were load-bearing for this architecture — and what I would do differently if each were wrong. (Design *decisions* — safe-routing over max automation, evidence-grounded extraction, never-downgrade, queue-and-worker over Kafka — live in the design document where they can be defended as choices; this log is reserved for things about the world I could not verify.)

1. **Referral letters arrive as machine-readable digital text** (dictated → transcribed → electronic, or typed). *If wrong* — if a meaningful fraction are scans or faxes — an OCR stage with its own confidence scoring joins ingestion, and OCR uncertainty must propagate into routing decisions: a second uncertainty source the policy layer has to compose, not ignore.

2. **The NICE 2WW criteria (and equivalent urgency indicators) are sufficiently rule-encodable to form a deterministic safety floor.** *If wrong* — if clinical reality is too contextual for clean rules — the safety floor shifts from "rules force escalation" to "a much larger share of letters held in human review," and Tier 1 coverage grows more slowly. The architecture survives; the coverage projections don't.

3. **Historical human decisions are useful but imperfect ground truth, and a few months of assistive-mode operation yields enough labelled decisions to calibrate confidence.** The brief itself notes priority varies by reviewer, so inter-reviewer consistency is an open question. *If wrong* — if reviewer disagreement is high or volume too low — automation stays gated longer, and the first deliverable becomes *measuring* reviewer agreement rather than exceeding it. The system cannot be more consistent than the standard it's trained to meet until that standard is itself understood.

4. **Redacted/pseudonymised text is sufficient for triage decisions, provided clinically load-bearing attributes (age, sex, relevant history) are preserved while identifiers are stripped.** *If wrong* — if redaction destroys decision-relevant signal — the privacy boundary moves: the pipeline processes identifiable data inside a restricted enclave instead, and the redaction layer applies to storage and review surfaces rather than to the model input.

5. **Clinical governance will accept confidence-gated automation of Routine referrals at all.** *If wrong* — if every letter must have human eyes indefinitely — the system's value proposition becomes permanently the MVP: speed, consistency, and urgency-ordered queues, with Tier 1 machinery dormant. Worth knowing before building it, which is why thresholds are config, not architecture.

6. **One trust, one specialty taxonomy, one set of local pathways at launch.** Multi-site is designed for (policy-as-config) but not built. *If wrong* — if multi-site arrives early — the work is configuration authoring and per-site eval suites, not re-architecture; that was the point of the config decision.

7. **The clinical admin team remains in post and engaged.** The feedback loop is powered by their corrections. *If wrong* — if the team shrinks on the promise of automation before coverage has actually grown — the system loses its safety net and its training signal simultaneously. Staffing decisions must be sequenced against coverage milestones; this is an operational dependency, not a technical one.

## POC implementation decisions

Smaller choices made during the build that are worth defending in the debrief.

**Negation: token-window, not regex.** `detect_red_flags` checks the 4 tokens immediately before each phrase match for negation words (`no`, `not`, `nil`, `denies`, etc.). This keeps the logic readable and auditable — the behaviour is fully visible in the test cases. REF-007 ("No frank rectal bleeding") and REF-008 ("no chest pain at rest") are the regression tests for this. *If wrong* — if real GP letters use negation patterns that defeat a fixed window (double negation, distant qualifiers) — the extraction layer needs a real NLP negation model; the interface stays the same, the implementation swaps.

**Hedged weight loss is not a red flag in `rules.py`.** Phrases like "may be related to reduced appetite" are intentionally not detected by keyword matching. The mock extractor (`extract.py`) is responsible for that clinical distinction. REF-004 and REF-007 carry weight-loss language but expect `URGENT`, not `TWO_WEEK_WAIT` — the gold labels hold because the extractor is not supposed to fire on qualified language. (REF-004 reaches its `URGENT` gold not through any extracted signal — the hedged weight loss is correctly suppressed — but through the GP-stated-priority floor in `policy.py`; the GP marked it urgent, and the floor forbids dropping below that.) *If wrong* — if the real LLM extracts hedged weight loss as a red flag anyway — the expected_priority labels become unreachable and the eval priority-accuracy metric will show it.

**`expected_priority` in synthetic data reflects the correct post-rule clinical answer.** Gold labels are not what the model alone would say — they are what the full pipeline (model + rules) should produce. This means calibration measurement on the classifier in isolation will show some priority "misses" that are actually correct escalations. The eval script must distinguish pre-rule model accuracy from post-rule pipeline accuracy.

**Thresholds are asserted, not calibrated.** `min_extraction_confidence: 0.70`, `min_specialty_confidence: 0.80`, `min_priority_confidence: 0.80` are starting values chosen to be conservative. They are not derived from a labelled holdout set. Calibration — checking that a 0.9 confidence score is correct ~90% of the time — is the single biggest gap between this POC and something trustworthy.

**Rectal bleeding + weight loss → `TWO_WEEK_WAIT` is a combination rule, not two individual rules.** Either flag alone reaches `URGENT`; together they hit the lower-GI cancer pathway ceiling. This reflects NICE guidance and is encoded explicitly in `_TWO_WEEK_WAIT_COMBINATIONS` in `rules.py`, not implied by threshold logic.

**A schema contract guarantees shape, not value semantics — proven by swapping in a real LLM extractor.** `extract.py` has a real Claude backend (`--real`) behind the same `ClinicalSignals` schema as the mock. Running it confirmed the structural modularity (data flowed through rules/policy/audit untouched, no crashes) but exposed that `classify.py` was implicitly coupled to the *exact symptom strings* the mock emits, not just the schema type. The real LLM's richer phrasings fell through to `Unknown` → HUMAN_REVIEW. *If wrong* — i.e. if this coupling is ignored — every backend swap silently degrades routing while looking type-safe. The real fix is a shared controlled vocabulary (enum/ontology) the extractor maps into, validated by the eval harness. Note the failure direction was safe: everything degraded to HUMAN_REVIEW, never to unsafe auto-route. This is the clearest single piece of evidence that the model is a swappable dependency and the architecture is the actual product.

**Update — the coupling fix was implemented (`canonicalize.py`).** A thin controlled-vocabulary layer now sits between extraction and classification, mapping free-text terms onto a fixed set the classifier keys off. Mock and real-LLM backends route the shared, unambiguous cases identically (the mock's distribution is 2 auto / 6 review / 2 exception; the real LLM is sampled, so it can vary on borderline letters — always toward review, never toward an unsafe auto-route). Implementing it surfaced a second-order bug — a naive substring map sent "lower GI" to Gastroenterology because the token "gi" matched first — caught by the canonicalisation tests, which is itself the point: controlled vocabularies need their own tests. In production this seam is where SNOMED CT would sit.

**Update — a sharper instance of the same hazard, caught in review.** Routing red flags through the *symptom* vocabulary collapsed "changing mole" → "mole"; because "mole" is not a Two-Week Wait flag name, `red_flag_priority_floor` silently dropped from Two-Week Wait to Routine — a canonicalisation layer *upstream* of `rules.py` quietly de-escalating a flag, directly against the claim that the deterministic floor can only escalate or hold. It never produced an unsafe auto-route (any red flag still forces human review), and the dataset masked it because REF-002 carries other surviving flags — which is exactly why it needed a test, not a demo, to surface. Fixed by giving red flags a *separate* vocabulary whose canonical names are the ones `rules.py` owns, pinned by a regression test asserting the 2WW floor survives canonicalisation. The lesson generalises: a value-mapping layer must canonicalise *into* the vocabulary each downstream consumer owns, never a neighbouring one.

**Update — gate order corrected to safety-first.** The policy gate originally ran the confidence gates before the red-flag floor, so a red flag co-occurring with sub-threshold confidence exited reporting only "low confidence" — the flag absent from `rules_fired`, and its priority-floor escalation never applied. That contradicted both the design document's stated order and the non-negotiable that anything affecting routing is visible in `rules_fired`. The floor now runs immediately after the input pre-filter, before any confidence gate. Auto-route safety was never at risk either way (a red flag can never reach auto-route), but the audit trail now names the flag. REF-007 is the live example: it now reports its weight-loss red flag instead of a bare confidence number.

**Update — GP-stated priority enforced as a floor (`gp_stated_priority_floor`).** The design always claimed the system may never auto-downgrade below the GP's stated urgency, but nothing in the code read `referral.gp_stated_priority` — so REF-004 (GP-marked urgent, hedged weight loss correctly suppressed, model proposed Routine) silently auto-routed as Routine, the exact forbidden downgrade. The policy gate now applies the GP's stated priority as a second floor, in its own block with its own audit reason (distinct from the red-flag floor: "GP said urgent" is a different story from "red flag detected"). Two deliberate narrowings: it only fires when the model *proposed* a priority (an abstaining `UNKNOWN` proposal belongs to the unknown gate, not a phantom downgrade), and only when the GP outranks that proposal — `Priority.UNKNOWN` ranks 0, so a missing GP priority never fires. This took the Urgent/2WW downgrade count to 0 honestly. *Known narrow gap:* when a red flag is also present the red-flag block returns first, so a GP priority *higher* than the red-flag floor would not be separately applied to `recommended_priority` (the case still goes to human review — never an unsafe auto-route — but the displayed priority could understate the GP). No synthetic case exercises this; folding a combined `priority_max(red_flag_floor, gp_floor)` into the red-flag block, named in `rules_fired`, is the clean closure if wanted.

**Every clinical mapping and confidence value is an unvalidated assumption, flagged as such in code.** Two distinct layers: (1) the red-flag and signal→specialty/priority mappings (e.g. "chest pain → Cardiology/Urgent") are engineering placeholders authored without clinical sign-off — illustrative simplifications of NICE-style criteria, not a clinical ruleset; (2) every confidence number (0.90, 0.85, 0.30...) is a hand-picked constant, not a calibrated probability — a 0.90 does not mean "right 90% of the time". *If wrong* — and the rules definitely have gaps, e.g. isolated rectal bleeding should reach 2WW but currently only reaches Urgent — the failures surface as recall misses in the eval, not as silent unsafe routes, because the never-downgrade floor still holds. In production the clinical tables are owned by a clinical safety officer as versioned reviewed config, and the confidence values come from a calibrated model validated against a labelled holdout set. The architecture isolates both so that handoff is config-and-review, not a rewrite.
